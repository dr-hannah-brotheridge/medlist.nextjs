paracetamolsafety info only(show all)

( acetaminophen )
Drug action

Paracetamol is thought to modulate inhibitory descending serotonergic pathways, preventing transmission of nociceptive signals from the peripheral tissues to the spinal cord. It may also reduce prostaglandin production by inhibiting CNS cyclo-oxygenase pathways, resulting in reduced pain sensation. Inhibition of prostaglandins and endogenous pyrogens in the hypothalamus contribute to its anti-pyretic effect. Paracetamol does not inhibit peripheral cyclo-oxygenase pathways so has negligible anti-inflammatory effect.

Indications
mild to moderate pain; pyrexia with discomfort
Contra-indications
severe hepatic impairment (see Hepatic impairment below)
Cautions
individuals with risk factors for paracetamol-induced hepatotoxicity—risk of hepatotoxicity at standard therapeutic doses, see below; concomitant enzyme inducing drugs (may increase levels of hepatotoxic metabolites—see Interactions checker); risk of high anion gap metabolic acidosis in those taking paracetamol long-term with additional risk factors such as severe illness (including renal impairment, sepsis), malnutrition, alcoholism, or concomitant flucloxacillin use—see Paracetamol can cause high anion gap metabolic acidosis (HAGMA) Prescriber Update, December 2025; with intravenous use G6PD deficiency (increases risk of haemolytic anaemia, see Haemolytic anaemia - Sometimes caused by medicines Prescriber Update, December 2019)

Paracetamol-induced hepatotoxicity

Individuals with pre-existing hepatic impairment, or risk factors for depleted glutathione stores such as chronic malnutrition, dehydration, low body-mass index, or chronic excessive alcohol use may be at increased risk of developing hepatotoxicity at standard therapeutic doses; use with caution and reduce the dose.

Before administering, check when paracetamol was last administered, and check the cumulative paracetamol dose via all routes of administration over previous 24 hours. Overdosage with paracetamol is particularly dangerous as it may cause liver failure resulting in the need for liver transplant, or death. Hepatic damage is sometimes not apparent for 4 to 6 days (see Paracetamol poisoning).

Excipients

May contain sulfites; risk of hypersensitivity reaction especially in asthmatics—see also notes

Interactions
Stockley's alerts: paracetamol
Hepatic impairment

avoid in severe impairment or hepatic failure; in individuals with mild to moderate impairment the maximum daily dose should not exceed 60 mg/kg/day (maximum 2 g/day) for individuals with:

  • Body-weight less than 50 kg
  • Chronic, or compensated, active hepatic disease, especially those with mild to moderate hepatocellular insufficiency
  • Gilbert's syndrome
  • Other risk factors for hepatotoxicity (see Cautions above)
Renal impairment

use with caution in severe impairment; increase infusion dose interval to every 6 hours if creatinine clearance less than 30 mL/minute; note also sodium content of effervescent tablets (check product information)

Pregnancy

generally considered compatible for short-term use if clinically indicated, and is the drug of choice for short-term pain and fever management in all stages of pregnancy; severe pain or fever in pregnancy can harm the pregnant person and fetus if left untreated; limited information is available on long-term use in pregnancy; recent large studies have found no causal link between paracetamol use in pregnancy and an increased risk of intellectual disability, autism spectrum disorder, ADHD, or other neurodevelopmental disorders—see Paracetamol remains safe for pregnant women New Zealand College of Midwives, September 2025 and Paracetamol use during pregnancy Ministry of Health Manatū Hauora, September 2025

Breast-feeding
Compatible

Compatible

Either the drug is not excreted in clinically significant amounts into human breast milk or its use during lactation does not, or is not expected to, cause toxicity in a nursing infant.

Adverse effects

rarely malaise, skin reactions including Stevens-Johnson syndrome, toxic epidermal necrolysis, acute generalised exanthematous pustulosis (see Medsafe Prescriber Update June 2014—Paracetamol and Serious Skin Reactions); also reported blood disorders including thrombocytopenia (see Drug-Induced Immune Thrombocytopenia, Prescriber Update, March 2018), leucopenia, neutropenia, high anion gap metabolic acidosis (see Cautions above); also reported on infusion hypotension, flushing, tachycardia; important: liver damage (and less frequently renal damage) following overdosage, see Paracetamol poisoning

Report adverse reaction:
Dosing regimen

Funded products of different paracetamol oral liquid strengths (120 mg/5 mL and 250 mg/5 mL) may be the same colour and/or flavour. Be aware of these potential similarities and do not rely on the colour, smell, or flavour of a product to determine strength when dispensing or administering paracetamol, this will help to avoid dosing errors. This should also be highlighted to patients or caregivers—see Patient advice below.

Dose reduction with hepatotoxicity risk factors

For individuals with pre-existing hepatic impairment, or risk factors for hepatotoxicity, dose adjustment is required—see Paracetamol-induced hepatotoxicity in Cautions, and Hepatic impairment above, see also Medicine Data Sheet

Patient advice
  • Do not take with any other medications that contain paracetamol (including those purchased without prescription).
  • Seek medical advice immediately if you experience any serious skin reaction including severe rash, skin peeling, or mouth ulcers. 

If using the oral liquid: 

Be aware that paracetamol is available in different strengths. Different liquid medicines may have the same colour and flavour. Always read the label on the medicine container and do not rely on the colour, smell, or flavour to determine contents. If you are unsure, check the correct volume to measure with your pharmacist or a healthcare provider; it is recommended that a syringe is used to measure the dose.
